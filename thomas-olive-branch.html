What we’re actually trying to figure out

Not “past = present.” Slavery ended. That’s a huge structural break.

But one economic question survives:

If people can’t be owned, how is the value they create captured and distributed?

To talk cleanly, we separate:

  • Physical assets (stuff)
  • Ownership structures (titles/claims)
  • Labor (human time/skill/ideas)

Our shared baseline

  • People own themselves.
  • People can sell labor, ideas, products.
  • Centralized force can be dangerous.
  • Coordination is sometimes necessary (infrastructure, defense, disasters).
Freedom Coordination Abuse risk
Goal: keep this framed as “risk management + coordination,” not labels. Invite agreement first.

Before abolition: labor was treated as a direct asset

Under slavery, people were listed on balance sheets alongside land and equipment.

  • Land
  • Infrastructure
  • Equipment
  • Human labor capacity (owned)

Abolition removed the category “people as capital.”

Keep it factual. Emphasize: moral and legal transformation is real. The question is what replaced the extraction interface.

After abolition: ownership shifted to the environment around workers

Industrial society reorganized value capture around physical systems.

Ownership concentrated in:

  • Factories
  • Railroads
  • Housing
  • Farms
  • Tools and machines
  • Energy infrastructure
  • Distribution networks

Workers were paid wages, but access to production depended on using assets someone else owned.

Shift: owning bodies → owning platforms of life/work.

The physical scale of civilization (not speculation)

If we ignore stock prices and ask what the physical world costs to replace:

  • Produced physical assets: ~$400–600T (buildings, factories, infrastructure, machinery, vehicles)
  • Land value: ~$200–400T
  • Total physical substrate: ~$700–900T

Rough ranges (estimates vary), but the point stands: the physical layer is finite, heavy, and slow-changing.

If Thomas pushes numbers: acknowledge uncertainty; emphasize conceptual use—physical layer vs claims layer.

Financial ownership sits on top of the physical layer

Markets create claims on physical assets and their income.

  • Stocks → claims on corporate assets/profits
  • Debt → claims on future income
  • Real estate funds → pooled housing ownership
  • Private equity → ownership/control of operating companies

Key idea: multiple financial claims can stack on the same physical asset.

That’s why financial wealth can grow faster than the material economy beneath it.

Layer 1: physical Layer 2: legal Layer 3: financial
“Claims can exceed things” is the conceptual punch. Use “warehouse with multiple liens + equity + derivatives” example if needed.

Where worker value goes today

  • Wages — direct compensation
  • Profits — captured by owners of companies/assets
  • Rents — payments for access (land, housing, infrastructure, platforms)
  • Financial returns — distributed to shareholders/funds/institutions

The shift isn’t “capture disappears.” It becomes mediated through ownership of systems rather than ownership of people.

This slide is the “mechanics” summary. It sets up the question: how much value should feed back into stability?

Investment firms: not everything — but key nodes

Investment firms increasingly hold strategic categories:

  • Housing portfolios
  • Warehouses/logistics
  • Hospitals/care facilities
  • Energy infrastructure
  • Manufacturing
  • Agricultural land
  • Data centers

Control doesn’t require owning everything.
It requires owning bottlenecks and nodes.

That’s why debates feel “everywhere”: housing, healthcare, energy, and logistics are upstream of wages.

Keep it neutral. Emphasize “node ownership” as a systems description, not villain narrative.

Where our conversation actually converges

We both want

  • Strong individual rights
  • Decentralized power where possible
  • Coordination where necessary
  • Infrastructure that expands opportunity
  • Systems that don’t require constant crisis

So the real question becomes

How much system productivity should feed back into baseline security so participation stays possible without coercion?

Different tools can serve that goal. The word choice (“redistribution,” “UBI”) is often where emotions attach.

This is the “olive branch.” Reframe as shared goal; disagreements = implementation constraints, not motives.

Words that describe the “structural fork” without ideology

Stability tools (examples)

  • Tax credits / negative income tax
  • Public services (healthcare, transit, education)
  • Profit sharing / employee ownership
  • Resource dividends (e.g., land/resource rents)
  • Baseline income (various designs)

Neutral framing

Not “taking from X.”
More like: allocating part of system output to prevent cascade failure.

The argument isn’t “markets vs no markets.” It’s how to keep a complex system free and stable.

Offer vocabulary: “stability dividend,” “social dividend,” “automation dividend,” “risk buffer,” “baseline floor.”

Bottom line

Abolition ended “humans as capital.”

But the allocation of value still depends on ownership of systems people must use.

The central modern question:
Who owns the systems — and how should their productivity be shared so society stays free, stable, and adaptable?

That’s the question we’re debating. Everything else is implementation detail and risk tolerance.

Close with invitation: “Let’s pick one failure mode (housing / automation / healthcare) and design constraints together.”
